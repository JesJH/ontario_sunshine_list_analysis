## Multiple Comparisons — Plain Language Walkthrough

**Why it's a problem first:**
Imagine you flip a fair coin and ask "is it biased?" at each flip. If you ask that question 100 times, pure chance will give you ~5 "yes" answers even though the coin is fair. Same thing here — if we test 100 job clusters for a gender gap, roughly 5 will *look* significant just by random noise.

### Option 1: No Correction
Run each test at p < 0.05, call anything below that significant.
- **Pro**: Catches every real effect
- **Con**: ~5% of your "significant" findings are noise — with 200 clusters that's ~10 false alarms
- **Verdict**: Fine for exploration, not for anything you'd share

### Option 2: Bonferroni
Divide your threshold by the number of tests. 100 clusters → each test must hit p < 0.0005 to count.
- **Pro**: Almost zero false positives
- **Con**: Extremely strict — if a real gap exists but is modest (e.g. women earn 8% less), Bonferroni might miss it entirely. It assumes all tests are independent, which they aren't (clusters overlap in sector, etc.)
- **Verdict**: Too conservative for exploratory analysis — designed for clinical trials where a false positive could harm someone

### Option 3: Holm-Bonferroni
A smarter sequential version of Bonferroni — sorts p-values smallest to largest and applies progressively relaxed thresholds.
- **Pro**: Strictly better than Bonferroni (finds more real effects, same false positive guarantee)
- **Con**: Still fairly conservative, still assumes independence
- **Verdict**: A middle ground if you want family-wise error control

### Option 4: Benjamini-Hochberg FDR (my recommendation)
Instead of "no false positives allowed," it says: "of everything I call significant, at most 5% are probably noise."
- If you find 40 significant clusters, you expect ~2 to be false. You don't know *which* 2, but the overall signal is trustworthy.
- **Pro**: Much more sensitive — finds real effects that Bonferroni would miss. Standard in genomics, social science, data journalism
- **Con**: You accept a small false positive rate, so individual findings need hedging ("significant at 5% FDR" rather than "definitively biased")
- **Verdict**: Right tool for this analysis — you're mapping patterns, not convicting anyone

**My recommendation: Benjamini-Hochberg at 5% FDR.** Report it transparently and let the effect sizes tell the real story — a cluster that clears BH-FDR *and* shows a 15% salary gap is a much stronger finding than one that barely clears the threshold with a 2% gap.

## NLP Clustering — Your Bootstrap Idea is Excellent

The bootstrap stability approach you described is exactly right and is sometimes called **consensus clustering** or **cluster stability analysis**. Here's how it fits together:

**Full K optimization pipeline:**
1. **Embed** job titles with a sentence transformer (e.g. `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` — fast, strong)
2. **Traditional metrics** across a range of K (e.g. K = 20 to 300):
   - Elbow plot on WCSS (within-cluster sum of squares)
   - Normalized WCSS / BCSS ratio
   - Silhouette score (how tight clusters are vs. how separated)
3. **Bootstrap stability** at candidate K values (e.g. the top 3-5 from step 2):
   - Resample 80% of unique job titles N times (e.g. N=100)
   - Cluster each sample, measure **Adjusted Rand Index (ARI)** between pairs of bootstrap clusterings
   - High mean ARI + low variance = stable, robust clusters
   - This directly answers: "does the categorization change when data changes?"
4. **Human review layer** — for the top stable K, show 10 random titles per cluster for a sanity check and optional merge/split
